## The Decision Behind the Number

When people think about Social Security, they often focus on a single figure: the monthly benefit amount printed on their statement. But that number is only part of the story. The age at which you decide to claim can change that amount dramatically, and once you commit to an early or late start, the effect generally lasts for the rest of your life.

Social Security gives you a window. You can claim as early as age 62, wait until your full retirement age, or delay all the way to age 70 in exchange for a larger monthly check. Understanding how each choice affects your income is the foundation of a good claiming decision.

## What "Full Retirement Age" Actually Means

Your full retirement age, or FRA, is the age at which you qualify for 100 percent of the benefit you earned based on your work history. For anyone born in 1960 or later, that age is 67. For those born earlier, it falls somewhere between 66 and 67, depending on the exact birth year.

FRA matters because it is the reference point for every adjustment. Claim before it, and your benefit is reduced. Claim after it, and your benefit grows. The Social Security Administration built the system this way to keep lifetime payouts roughly balanced across different claiming ages, but the results can vary widely from person to person.

## Claiming Early: The Cost of Starting at 62

Claiming at 62, the earliest possible age, is popular, but it comes with a permanent trade-off. Starting benefits before your full retirement age reduces the monthly amount, and that reduction does not disappear when you eventually reach FRA.

For someone with an FRA of 67, claiming at 62 can cut the monthly benefit by roughly 30 percent compared with waiting until 67. In practical terms, a benefit that would have been $2,000 a month at 67 could shrink to about $1,400 a month if claimed at 62. That difference continues for the rest of your life, and it also affects survivor benefits your spouse may later receive.

Even so, claiming early can make sense for people who need the cash flow, who have health concerns, or who simply prefer to receive income while they are younger and more active.

## Delaying Past FRA: Delayed Retirement Credits

On the other end, waiting past your full retirement age earns what are called delayed retirement credits. For each year you delay beyond FRA, up to age 70, your benefit increases by about 8 percent per year. There is no additional benefit for waiting past 70, so 70 is the practical ceiling.

Using the same example, a $2,000 monthly benefit at 67 could grow to roughly $2,480 a month by age 70. For those who expect a long retirement and can afford to wait, this guaranteed increase is difficult to match elsewhere.

## The Earnings Test: A Common Surprise

Many people plan to claim Social Security while continuing to work, and this is where an often-overlooked rule comes into play: the earnings test.

If you claim before your full retirement age and are still earning income from a job, Social Security may temporarily withhold part of your benefit once your earnings pass an annual threshold. This surprises a lot of new claimants who assume their check is theirs to keep regardless of other income.

The important word here is temporary. The withheld amounts are not lost forever. Once you reach full retirement age, Social Security recalculates your benefit to account for the months that were withheld, effectively giving that money back over time. The earnings test also disappears entirely at FRA, so working while collecting benefits after that age has no effect on your check.

## Factors That Shape the Right Age for You

Because the system is designed to be roughly neutral over an average lifespan, there is no single correct claiming age. The right choice depends on your personal circumstances.

**Health and life expectancy.** If you expect a long retirement, delaying can produce more total income over your lifetime. If your health is uncertain, claiming earlier may allow you to enjoy the money sooner.

**Cash flow needs.** Someone who needs income immediately after leaving work may not have the luxury of waiting, regardless of the larger future benefit.

**Spousal strategy.** Married couples have more moving parts. The higher earner's decision affects survivor benefits, so coordinating the two claiming ages can matter as much as either one alone.

**How long the income must last.** Social Security is inflation-adjusted and guaranteed for life, which makes it especially valuable as protection against outliving other savings.

## Putting the Pieces Together

Choosing when to claim Social Security is less about finding a universally best age and more about matching the decision to your own health, finances, and family situation. The monthly number on your statement is the starting point, not the conclusion. By understanding how early claiming reduces benefits, how delaying increases them, and how the earnings test can temporarily affect your checks, you can make a decision grounded in facts rather than assumptions.
