An advance directive is a legal document that communicates a person's wishes about medical care when they become unable to speak for themselves. A living will and a healthcare proxy are both types of advance directives — but they serve distinct functions.

## Three Documents, Three Functions

A **living will** — sometimes called a healthcare directive or directive to physicians — is an instructional document. It records specific preferences about medical treatment: life-sustaining treatment if in a persistent vegetative state, artificial nutrition and hydration, comfort and palliative care priorities, and similar decisions. A living will speaks to the situations it describes. It cannot address situations it did not anticipate.

A **healthcare proxy** — also called a durable power of attorney for health care — names a specific person to make medical decisions on your behalf when you cannot. Unlike a living will, a healthcare proxy can respond to unanticipated circumstances in real time. The proxy can consult with the medical team, ask questions, and exercise judgment.

A **comprehensive advance directive** combines both: it names a proxy and provides written guidance about treatment preferences. Some states have unified forms; others require separate instruments. Legal requirements — witnesses, notarization — vary by state.

## What Happens Without These Documents

When a person lacks an advance directive and becomes incapacitated, medical decision-making authority defaults to a **state law hierarchy**: legal spouse first, then adult children, then parents, then siblings. Unmarried domestic partners — regardless of the relationship's length — typically have no legal standing unless specifically named.

Disputes among family members, in the absence of documented instructions, can become legally complex and costly. In some cases, establishing decision-making authority requires court proceedings including guardianship hearings.

Research cited by the National Institute on Aging found that family members guessed nearly one in three end-of-life preferences incorrectly for their loved ones — even when they believed they knew those wishes. Written documentation removes that margin of error.

## POLST Forms: A Separate Layer

**Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST)** forms — or their state equivalents (MOLST, MOST, etc.) — are physician-signed medical orders that travel with a patient and provide immediately actionable instructions to emergency responders. A POLST is not a substitute for an advance directive, and vice versa. POLST forms are typically appropriate for individuals with serious illness or frailty.

## State-by-State Variation

Advance directive law is primarily a matter of state law. A document executed validly in one state may not meet the technical requirements of another. For individuals who divide time between states, the ABA Commission on Law and Aging and CaringInfo (a program of the National Hospice and Palliative Care Organization) maintain state-specific form information.

Most states provide standard forms that can be completed without an attorney.

## Reviewing and Updating Documents

The National Institute on Aging recommends reviewing advance directives at least annually and after significant life events: a change in health status, marital status, relocation to a different state, or a change in the named proxy's availability.

Copies should be held by the named proxy, your primary care physician, and any hospital or care facility involved in ongoing care.

State-specific forms are available at no cost through most state health departments and Area Agencies on Aging.
