## Why Couples Face a Different Decision Than Singles

When a single person decides when to claim Social Security, the math is relatively contained: claim earlier for a smaller monthly check that starts sooner, or wait for a larger one. For married couples, the picture is more layered. Two claiming decisions interact with each other, and two additional types of benefits—spousal and survivor—enter the equation. Those benefits can turn one spouse's claiming date into a decision that affects the couple's income today and the income of whichever spouse lives longer.

Understanding how these pieces fit together is one of the most valuable things a married couple can do before filing.

## Understanding the Spousal Benefit

Social Security allows a lower-earning spouse to receive a benefit based on the higher earner's work record. At full retirement age, this spousal benefit can be worth up to 50 percent of the higher earner's benefit calculated at their own full retirement age.

Full retirement age, often abbreviated FRA, is the age at which you qualify for 100 percent of your own earned benefit. For people born in 1960 or later, FRA is 67. For those born between 1943 and 1954, it was 66, with a gradual increase for birth years in between.

The 50 percent figure is a ceiling, not a guarantee. A spouse receives the full 50 percent only if they wait until their own full retirement age to claim the spousal benefit. Claiming earlier reduces it.

There is an important detail here: unlike a person's own retirement benefit, the spousal benefit does not grow past full retirement age. There are no delayed retirement credits on a spousal benefit. So while waiting until FRA maximizes it, waiting beyond FRA gains nothing on the spousal portion.

## Why Claiming Early Reduces the Spousal Benefit

If a spouse claims before their full retirement age, the reduction can be significant. The spousal benefit is permanently lowered based on how many months early the claim is made.

As an illustration, if the higher earner's full retirement benefit is $2,400 per month, the maximum spousal benefit at the lower earner's FRA would be $1,200. Claiming several years early could reduce that figure to well below $1,200, and that reduction lasts for life. Because these reductions are permanent, the early-claiming decision deserves careful thought, especially for a spouse who expects a long retirement.

## How Delayed Credits Grow the Higher Earner's Benefit

The higher earner in a couple has a powerful tool: delayed retirement credits. For every year they wait to claim past full retirement age, up to age 70, their own retirement benefit grows by roughly 8 percent per year. Someone with a full retirement benefit of $2,400 at age 67 could see that grow to about $2,976 by waiting until age 70—an increase of around 24 percent, and one that continues for the rest of their life, adjusted each year for inflation.

This is where the two decisions connect. The higher earner's claiming date does more than boost their own monthly check. Because of survivor rules, it also sets the floor for the income the surviving spouse will eventually receive.

## The Survivor Benefit: Often the Most Important Factor

The Social Security Administration notes that a surviving spouse is generally eligible for up to 100 percent of the deceased spouse's benefit—including any delayed retirement credits the deceased earned.

This is the piece many couples overlook. When one spouse dies, the household stops receiving the smaller of the two Social Security checks and keeps the larger one. So if the higher earner delayed until 70 and locked in a benefit of about $2,976, the surviving spouse can step into that higher amount, not the smaller benefit they were receiving on their own.

For many couples, this makes the higher earner's decision to wait less about maximizing income during both spouses' lifetimes and more about protecting the survivor. Since women, on average, outlive men, the surviving spouse is frequently the wife—and often the lower earner. Delaying the higher earner's claim can meaningfully raise the income she relies on for what could be many additional years.

## Putting the Pieces Together

A useful way to frame the decision is to separate the two roles each benefit plays:

- The **spousal benefit** matters most during both spouses' lifetimes. It is capped at 50 percent of the higher earner's FRA benefit and is maximized by claiming at full retirement age, not later.
- The **higher earner's own benefit** matters both now and later. Delaying it raises household income while both spouses are living and raises the survivor benefit after one dies.
- The **survivor benefit** is often the strongest reason to delay the higher earner's claim, because it can equal up to 100 percent of that benefit, including delayed credits.

For couples with a meaningful gap between their two earning records, coordinating these claiming dates—rather than treating each spouse's decision in isolation—can shape their financial security for decades.
