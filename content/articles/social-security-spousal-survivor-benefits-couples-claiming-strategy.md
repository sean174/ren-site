## Why Couples Face Extra Complexity

For married couples approaching retirement, Social Security is rarely a matter of one person deciding when to claim. The rules governing spousal and survivor benefits tie two people's decisions together, creating both complexity and genuine opportunity. A choice one spouse makes can raise or lower the lifetime income the other receives, sometimes by tens of thousands of dollars.

At the center of these rules is a figure called the primary insurance amount, or PIA. This is the monthly benefit a worker would receive if they claimed at their full retirement age, which for people born in 1960 or later is 67. The PIA is the anchor from which spousal and survivor benefits are calculated, so it helps to keep it in mind as the reference point for everything that follows.

## Understanding the Spousal Benefit

A spouse who has little or no earnings history of their own may still qualify for a Social Security benefit based on their partner's record. This spousal benefit can be worth up to 50 percent of the higher-earning spouse's PIA.

Consider a couple where the higher earner has a PIA of $2,400 per month. The lower-earning spouse could be eligible for a spousal benefit of up to $1,200 per month, half of that amount. If the lower earner also qualifies for a benefit on their own work record, Social Security effectively pays the higher of the two, not both stacked together.

The crucial detail is that the full 50 percent applies only when the lower-earning spouse claims at their own full retirement age. Claiming earlier reduces the spousal benefit on a sliding scale. Someone who files several years early may see their spousal benefit fall to roughly 32.5 to 35 percent of the higher earner's PIA rather than the full 50 percent.

There is also an important limitation on the upside. Unlike a worker's own retirement benefit, a spousal benefit earns no credit for waiting past full retirement age. A person's own benefit grows by roughly 8 percent per year, in the form of delayed retirement credits, for each year they postpone claiming between full retirement age and 70. The spousal benefit does not. Delaying a spousal claim beyond full retirement age simply means forgoing income with nothing gained in return.

## The Timing Interaction Between Two Spouses

Because both people's decisions are linked, the sequence of claims matters. A spousal benefit cannot begin until the higher-earning spouse has filed for their own benefit. This single rule shapes many couples' strategies.

A common approach works like this. The higher earner delays claiming, often to age 70, to maximize both their own monthly benefit and, as we will see, the survivor benefit that may eventually pass to the other spouse. Meanwhile, the lower-earning spouse claims earlier to bring some income into the household during the interim years.

There is a trade-off buried in this plan. Because the spousal benefit cannot start until the higher earner files, a lower-earning spouse who wants a spousal benefit may have to wait. In practice, many couples have the lower earner claim on their own record first, then switch to a spousal benefit once the higher earner files, though the availability of that maneuver depends on the specific figures and the ages involved.

## Survivor Benefits Raise the Stakes

Survivor benefits add another layer that often proves the most consequential of all. When one spouse dies, the surviving spouse is generally eligible to receive up to 100 percent of the deceased spouse's benefit, including any delayed retirement credits the higher earner accumulated.

This is why the higher earner's claiming age is so important. Suppose the higher earner has a PIA of $2,400 but delays until 70, growing the benefit to roughly $2,976. If that person dies first, the surviving spouse can step up to that larger amount, replacing their own smaller benefit. The delay the higher earner chose years earlier continues to pay off for the survivor, potentially for decades.

The survivor benefit does not stack on top of the survivor's own benefit. Instead, the survivor receives the larger of the two. For couples with a meaningful gap in earnings histories, protecting the survivor by maximizing the higher earner's benefit can be one of the most valuable financial moves available.

## Putting the Pieces Together

The interplay of these rules explains why the higher earner's claiming decision ranks among the most consequential financial choices a married couple makes. It affects the higher earner's own lifetime benefit, the spousal benefit available in the meantime, and the survivor benefit that may support one spouse alone for years.

Every couple's numbers differ, shaped by earnings records, health, ages, and other income. But understanding how spousal and survivor benefits connect gives couples a clearer picture of what is at stake before they decide when each person should claim.
