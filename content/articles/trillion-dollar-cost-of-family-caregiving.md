## The Care Already Happening in Millions of Homes

When most people imagine long-term care, they picture a nursing home, a distant possibility that might surface many years from now. But a quieter form of care is already unfolding across the country every day. It isn't provided by nurses or facilities. It's provided by spouses, adult children, siblings, and neighbors who quietly rearrange their own lives to help someone they love.

This is the part of long-term care that rarely shows up in a family's planning conversations, and yet it may be the single largest expense many households will ever absorb, often without recognizing it as an expense at all.

## A $1.01 Trillion Figure

According to a 2026 report from the AARP Public Policy Institute, roughly 59 million Americans provided unpaid care for an adult family member, friend, or neighbor in 2024. When researchers assigned a dollar value to all those hours, the total came to an estimated $1.01 trillion annually.

To put that in perspective, that figure exceeds total Medicaid spending across all levels of government. Medicaid is the joint federal and state program that pays for a large share of the nation's formal long-term care. In other words, the care families provide for free is worth more than the country's largest public program dedicated to health and long-term care combined.

That number matters because it makes visible something families almost never account for: the enormous value they quietly expect to supply themselves.

## The Costs That Never Reach an Invoice

Unpaid caregiving does not mean cost-free caregiving. The price is simply paid in ways that don't appear on any bill.

Consider a daughter who reduces her work hours to drive a parent to medical appointments. She loses income today, but she may also lose future raises, promotions, and the compounding growth of retirement contributions she can no longer make. Consider a son who postpones his own retirement to help a parent, trading years he expected to spend on his own life. Consider a spouse whose physical health declines under the daily strain of lifting, bathing, and monitoring a partner around the clock.

None of these costs generate a receipt. There is no statement showing the promotions not earned or the Social Security benefits reduced by fewer years of higher earnings. But the losses are real, and they can quietly reshape a caregiver's own retirement security.

The emotional and physical toll adds another layer. Caregivers report higher rates of stress, sleep disruption, and their own health problems. When a caregiver's health breaks down, a single family situation can become two.

## What Professional Care Actually Costs

The reason so many families lean on unpaid caregiving becomes clearer when you look at the price of the professional alternative.

According to the 2025 CareScout Cost of Care Survey, the national median cost of assisted living reached $74,400 per year in 2025. Assisted living generally serves people who need help with daily activities such as dressing, bathing, and managing medications, but who do not require round-the-clock medical care.

For those who need a higher level of care, the figures climb sharply. A semi-private room in a nursing home ran a national median of $114,975 per year in 2025. A semi-private room means the resident shares living space with another person; private rooms cost more still.

These are median figures, meaning half of facilities cost less and half cost more. Prices vary widely by region, with many coastal and metropolitan areas running well above the national midpoint. Faced with numbers like these, many families conclude that providing care themselves is the only affordable path, even when the hidden costs eventually rival the price of paid care.

## Why Planning Ahead Changes the Options

The central lesson in these figures is not that families should avoid caregiving. For many, caring for a loved one is a deeply meaningful choice they would make again. The lesson is that families rarely plan for the full scope of what care requires, and that gap tends to surface during a crisis, when choices are fewest and emotions are highest.

Planning ahead widens the range of realistic options. Some families explore long-term care insurance, which can help cover the cost of assisted living, in-home aides, or nursing home care, though premiums and eligibility depend on age and health at the time of purchase. Others build dedicated savings earmarked for care. Some use legal structures such as trusts to organize assets and clarify how care will be funded and managed.

Just as important is the conversation itself. Discussing who might provide care, how much time it would demand, and what it would cost, before a health event forces the issue, allows a family to distribute responsibility more fairly and to recognize the sacrifices involved rather than assuming they will simply be absorbed.

## Seeing the Whole Picture

The $1.01 trillion figure is ultimately a reminder that long-term care is already a household reality for tens of millions of Americans. It is not a distant event reserved for nursing homes. Understanding both the visible price of professional care and the invisible price of family care allows families to prepare for the full weight of what caregiving asks, on time and on their own terms.
